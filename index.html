Pure Mathematics

Logic and Foundation
  1. Set theory: The study of well-defined collections of objects.
  2. Mathematical Logic: The study of formal reasoning.
  3. Model Theory: The relationship between formal languages and mathematical structures.
Arthematic

The most fundamental branch of mathematics, dealing with numbers and the basic ways they can be combined.

Abstract Algebra
  1. Group theory: The study of symmetry and structure.
  2. Ring theory: Structures involving addition and multiplication.
  3. Field Theory: The study of properties of algebraic fields.
Number Theory
  1. Elementary Number Theory:

    Focuses on the properties of integers, especially prime numbers. It deals with concepts like divisibility, the Euclidean Algorithm, and the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

  2. Analytic Number Theory:

    Uses methods from calculus and complex analysis to solve problems about integers. A famous example is the Prime Number Theorem, which describes the distribution of primes.

  3. Algebraic Number Theory:

    Generalizes the concept of numbers to algebraic structures called number fields. It studies "algebraic integers" and is crucial for solving equations like Fermat's Last Theorem.

  4. Applications in Cryptography:

    This is where Number Theory meets the real world. Algorithms like RSA encryption rely on the extreme difficulty of factoring large composite numbers into their prime components.

"Mathematics is the queen of the sciences, and number theory is the queen of mathematics." — Carl Friedrich Gauss
Geomtry
  1. Euclidean geometry:

    the basic geometry about triangles and all that kinda of stuff that we see in high school

  2. Non-Euclidean geometry:

    The study of the geometrical properties of hyperboloids and some of the more advanced shapes

  3. Trignometery:

    A sub-branch that focuses specifically on the relationships between the side lengths and angles of triangles.

  4. Differential Geometry:

    a branch of mathematics that uses the tools of calculus and linear algebra to study the geometry of smooth curves, surfaces, and higher-dimensional spaces.

Topology
  1. general topology:

    The foundational language of the field. It studies set-theoretic definitions and concepts of "closeness" and continuity, including properties like compactness, connectedness, and separation axioms. It examines exactly what constitutes a topological space without focusing on complex geometric shapes

  2. Algebraic Topology:

    Translates difficult geometric or topological problems into algebraic problems that are easier to compute. It uses algebraic concepts (like groups and rings) to distinguish between topological spaces by examining their global properties, holes, and connectivity. Common tools include homology and homotopy groups.

  3. Differential Topology:

    Studies differentiable functions and manifolds. It applies the methods of calculus to study spatial structures that possess "smoothness" at every point, analyzing characteristics like vector fields, intersections, and smooth mappings.

  4. Geometric Topology:

    Focuses specifically on the study of manifolds and their embeddings in lower-dimensional spaces (like 3-manifolds and 4-manifolds). It deals with knots, links, and the ways objects can sit inside another space.

  5. Applied Topology :

    Applies topological principles to real-world data and physical systems. A prominent subfield is Topological Data Analysis (TDA), which uses concepts like persistent homology to extract structural features and shapes from complex, high-dimensional datasets in fields like biology, robotics, and artificial intelligence.

Mathematical analysis
  1. Real analysis:

    The rigorous study of real numbers, sequences, limits, continuity, differentiation, and integration. It establishes the theoretical foundation for standard calculus.

  2. Complex analysis:

    The study of functions of complex numbers. It heavily utilizes complex differentiability (analyticity) and contour integration, offering powerful tools with applications in fluid dynamics and electrodynamics.

  3. functional analysis:

    An extension of real and complex analysis that studies infinite-dimensional vector spaces (function spaces) and linear operators acting upon them. It provides the mathematical framework for quantum mechanics.

    >
  4. measure theory:

    A generalization of classical integration and length/area/volume. It provides the mathematical foundations for probability theory and Lebesgue integration

Applied Mathematics

Differential Equations

The language of physics, describing how quantities change. Essential for modeling planetary motion or heat flow.

Control Theory

The heart of Mechatronics! It deals with managing the behavior of dynamic systems using feedback loops.

Discrete Mathematics

The study of mathematical structures that are fundamentally discrete rather than continuous. It includes graph theory and logic, forming the backbone of Computer Science.

Numerical Analysis

The study of algorithms that use numerical approximation for the problems of mathematical analysis. This is essential when exact solutions are impossible to find by hand, especially in computer simulations.

Optimization

The selection of a best element from some set of available alternatives. In engineering, this means finding the most efficient way to build a bridge or the fastest route for a delivery drone.

Mathematical Modeling

The process of representing real-world systems using mathematical concepts. It bridges the gap between theoretical math and practical sciences like biology, economics, and sociology.

Computational Mathematics

Involves mathematical research in areas of science where computing plays a central and essential role, emphasizing algorithms, numerical methods, and symbolic computations.

Probability & Statistics

The mathematics of uncertainty and data. Probability predicts the likelihood of future events, while statistics analyzes the frequency of past events to find patterns.

Mathematical Physics

Applying the rigor of pure mathematics to theoretical physics problems like General Relativity and Quantum Mechanics.
